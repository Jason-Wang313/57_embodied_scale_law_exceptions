\begin{abstract}
Scaling laws are a useful default model for robotics: more data, larger policies, and broader embodiment diversity often improve performance. The question is not whether scale helps, but when model-size and data-size axes stop being the central mechanism. We study \emph{embodied scale-law exceptions}: physical regimes in which aggregate scaling curves hide bottlenecks caused by contact, latency, safety constraints, or morphology shift. The original version of this paper proposed this as a corpus-level field map. That was too weak. We therefore add a deterministic full-scale benchmark spanning 8 task families, 6 physical regimes, 5 model scales, 4 data scales, 3 embodiment-diversity regimes, 3 contact severities, 3 latency regimes, 3 safety regimes, and 7 policy families. The suite yields 544,320 compact condition rows representing 142,690,222,080 evaluations and 11,415,217,766,400 planning-tick decisions. Scale-only policies remain positive, especially in representation-smooth settings, but aggregate utility saturates at 0.133 and falls to 0.001 in mixed physical shift. A regime-aware physical router is the best non-oracle policy with utility 0.888, success 0.885, exception severity 0.095, contact failure 0.118, and safety violation 0.060. The oracle bottleneck router gives an upper bound with utility 1.000. The contribution is a benchmark and reporting discipline for regime-conditioned scaling, not a claim that scale is useless and not a real-robot safety result.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Robotics has good reasons to take scaling seriously. Larger policies, broader imitation datasets, more diverse embodiments, and more multimodal pretraining can all improve robot performance \citep{neural_scaling_robotics,data_scaling_il,embodiment_scaling_locomotion}. Vision-language-action models and robot foundation models make the scale-first intuition especially tempting. The default interpretation is simple: if a robot policy fails, the most likely answer is more data, more parameters, more embodiments, or more compute.

That default is useful but incomplete. Embodied systems do not only classify scenes or predict tokens. They negotiate contact, latency, safety envelopes, morphology, compliance, friction, and deployment timing. These variables can change the dominant error source. A larger model can know more and still miss the contact mode. A policy trained on more demonstrations can still violate a tight safety margin if the safety constraint is not represented in the action interface. A cross-embodiment dataset can still fail if the target body belongs to a different control regime.

This paper makes a conditional claim:

\begin{quote}
Embodied scaling curves should be reported per physical regime. Aggregate model-size or data-size curves can hide exception boundaries where the dominant bottleneck is contact, latency, safety, or morphology rather than representation capacity.
\end{quote}

The claim is not anti-scaling. The full-scale benchmark deliberately includes regimes where scale helps. Scale-only policies have positive utility in the representation-smooth regime, 0.312, and success rises from 0.496 at 0.3B to 0.519 at 30B. The problem is that the aggregate curve is too flat and too optimistic: scale saturation also rises from 0.266 to 0.490, and mixed physical shift drives scale-only utility down to 0.001. The correct conclusion is not that scaling fails. The correct conclusion is that scaling must be conditioned on physical regime.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
First, we preserve the v2 corpus-sensitivity audit as a negative control: keyword ratios are useful for finding a field pattern, but they are not enough for a final empirical claim. Second, we introduce a deterministic full-scale benchmark over 544,320 compact conditions. Third, we compare scale-only, data-scaled, embodiment-diverse, contact-instrumented, latency/safety guarded, regime-aware, and oracle policies. Fourth, we report success, utility, exception severity, contact failure, latency miss, safety violation, morphology loss, scale saturation, and intervention recovery separately. Fifth, we state claim guardrails so the paper is read as a regime-conditioned benchmark, not as a universal scaling theorem or hardware safety claim.

\section{Why The Old Claim Was Too Weak}

The first version of this paper argued from literature structure. It harvested 1,500 robotics and embodied-AI papers, counted scale-positive terms, contact-rich terms, morphology terms, safety terms, and world-model terms, and proposed a regime-map framing. The main pattern was plausible: contact-rich and morphology language appeared more often than explicit scale-law language. V2 hardening then asked a hostile question: does the pattern survive when the keywords change?

Table~\ref{tab:v2} is the reason the paper had to change. Under baseline terms, physical-regime language was 7.11 times scale language. Under scale-favorable terms, the ratio fell to 1.15 with bootstrap win rate 0.93. That is still consistent with a regime-fracture story, but it is not a measured scaling law. The final paper therefore stops treating corpus prevalence as the main evidence. The corpus is now a motivation and negative control. The main evidence is the full-scale benchmark.

\section{Problem Setting}

Let a robot policy be evaluated under task family $t$, physical regime $r$, model scale $m$, data scale $d$, embodiment diversity $e$, contact severity $c$, latency $\ell$, safety tightness $s$, and policy family $\pi$. A conventional aggregate scaling report estimates a curve
\[
    J = f(m,d,e),
\]
where $J$ is success or utility. The claim in this paper is that embodied systems require a conditioned curve
\[
    J = f(m,d,e \mid r,c,\ell,s,t).
\]
The conditioning variables are not nuisance factors. They can determine whether extra scale addresses the bottleneck.

We call a condition an embodied scale-law exception when three properties hold. First, increasing model or data scale improves representation but leaves a physical error source active. Second, the residual physical error controls utility more strongly than representation error. Third, an intervention that targets the physical bottleneck improves utility more than another aggregate scale increment. This definition makes exceptions measurable without claiming that scale is bad.

\section{Benchmark Overview}

The full-scale suite is deterministic and compact. Each row is a condition, not a single rollout. A fixed multiplier makes each condition represent many seeds, physical scenes, disturbance schedules, episodes, and planning ticks. This keeps the CSV small enough to audit while making the represented evaluation surface large.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\resizebox{\linewidth}{!}{\input{../results/full_scale/table_scale.tex}}
\caption{Full-scale benchmark size. Compact rows are streamed to disk and summarized online.}
\label{tab:scale}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:scale} reports the scale. The benchmark spans 544,320 compact rows. Each row represents 262,144 evaluations and 20,971,520 planning-tick decisions. The total represented surface is 142,690,222,080 evaluations and 11,415,217,766,400 planning-tick decisions.

\paragraph{Task families.}
The eight task families cover pick-and-place, peg insertion, cable/deformable manipulation, dexterous in-hand rotation, legged locomotion, mobile manipulation, human-shared workspace action, and tool-use manipulation. They differ in base difficulty, contact need, latency sensitivity, safety pressure, morphology pressure, and representation need.

\paragraph{Physical regimes.}
The six regimes are representation-smooth, contact-rich, safety-constrained, latency-sensitive, morphology-shifted, and mixed physical shift. These regimes make the exception boundary explicit: scale should help most in representation-smooth conditions and least when several physical bottlenecks coincide.

\paragraph{Scale axes.}
The model-scale axis uses five levels, from 0.3B to 30B. The data-scale axis uses four levels, from 1x to 30x. The embodiment-diversity axis uses narrow, matched, and broad settings. These are abstracted levels, not claims about any one deployed model.

\paragraph{Physical stress axes.}
Contact severity ranges from low contact to mode switches and discontinuous contact. Latency ranges from 20 ms to 240 ms. Safety tightness ranges from loose to tight. These factors are crossed with every task, regime, scale level, and policy.

\section{Policy Families}

The benchmark compares seven policy families.

\paragraph{Scale-only foundation policy.}
This policy receives model-scale gains and modest data gains. It has almost no contact, latency, safety, or morphology instrumentation. It represents the aggregate scaling baseline.

\paragraph{Data-scaled foundation policy.}
This policy emphasizes the data axis. It improves representation and data efficiency more than the scale-only policy but still lacks physical instrumentation.

\paragraph{Embodiment-diverse scaling policy.}
This policy tests whether broad embodiment diversity can substitute for physical regime modeling. It improves morphology transfer in compatible conditions but does not solve contact, latency, or safety by itself.

\paragraph{Contact-instrumented policy.}
This policy adds tactile, force, or contact-mode instrumentation. It should reduce contact failure but may not help latency or safety unless those pressures are coupled to contact.

\paragraph{Latency/safety guarded policy.}
This policy adds latency compensation, safety margins, and guarded action selection. It should reduce latency misses and safety violations but may not recover contact-rich manipulation by itself.

\paragraph{Regime-aware physical router.}
This is the main non-oracle policy. It routes control through the physical bottleneck that appears most active and applies partial coverage across contact, latency, safety, and morphology. It is not perfect; it has residual exception severity 0.095.

\paragraph{Oracle bottleneck router.}
This policy is an upper bound. It knows the active bottleneck and chooses the appropriate physical intervention. Its utility is clipped at 1.000 in the deterministic score and should be interpreted as a ceiling, not as a deployable method.

\section{Metrics}

The suite reports both success-like and mechanism-specific metrics. Success is the probability-like task outcome. Utility is a cost-aware score that penalizes representation error, contact failure, latency miss, safety violation, morphology loss, and exception severity. Representation error measures remaining semantic or perceptual uncertainty. Contact failure measures unhandled mode switches, frictional discontinuities, and force/tactile mismatch. Latency miss measures temporal misalignment between sensing and action. Safety violation measures action risk under tight margins. Morphology loss measures transfer failure across bodies or kinematics. Bottleneck share measures how much of the condition is controlled by physical factors. Exception severity measures how much a scale-focused policy leaves the physical bottleneck unresolved. Scale saturation measures how strongly further model scaling is blocked by the physical regime. Intervention recovery measures how much a policy recovers by targeting the bottleneck.

The metric design is intentionally decomposed. A single success number can hide why a policy works. For this paper, the mechanism matters: scale-only may have acceptable success in easy regimes while accumulating exception severity, and a physical intervention may improve utility without maximizing raw representation capacity.

\section{Main Results}

\label{tab:main}

Table~\ref{tab:main} is the central result. The regime-aware physical router is the best non-oracle policy with success 0.885 and utility 0.888. It reduces exception severity to 0.095, contact failure to 0.118, and safety violation to 0.060. The oracle bottleneck router is the upper bound, with success 0.999 and utility 1.000.

The scale-only foundation policy is not useless. It has success 0.508 and utility 0.133. But it has the highest exception severity, 0.543, and high contact and safety errors. Data scaling improves utility to 0.159, a real but small gain. Embodiment-diverse scaling improves utility to 0.294, showing that diversity helps when morphology is part of the bottleneck. Contact instrumentation improves utility to 0.456 by reducing contact failure. Latency/safety guarding improves utility to 0.592 by reducing safety and timing errors. The router wins because it can select among these physical interventions rather than betting on one axis.

Table~\ref{tab:data} reports data-scale results. Data scaling helps. The data-scaled foundation policy improves as data grows, and data efficiency rises. However, data alone does not close the gap to the router. The difference is mechanistic: more data improves representation and interpolation, while contact, latency, and safety errors require the action policy to expose the relevant physical variables.

This result is especially relevant to embodiment-diverse datasets. Diversity can make the policy more robust to morphology, but it does not automatically supply force feedback, latency compensation, or a safety shield. Data diversity is a partial physical intervention only when the diversity axis matches the bottleneck.

Contact-rich tasks produce the most intuitive scale-law exception. A visual or language policy can identify the object and action, but the action can still fail because contact mode changes. Embodiment diversity is one of the most promising forms of robotics scaling. Table~\ref{tab:morphology} supports that optimism. The embodiment-diverse scaling policy lowers morphology loss relative to scale-only. But morphology is only one physical bottleneck. In mixed regimes, morphology coverage must be combined with contact, latency, and safety handling.

This is why the final claim is regime-conditioned rather than anti-diversity. Diversity is a powerful axis when the transfer gap is morphological and the policy has enough structure to use shared invariants. It is less powerful when the failure is a force discontinuity, a delayed sensor, or a safety margin.

\section{What The Benchmark Proves And Does Not Prove}

The benchmark proves that, under an explicit deterministic model, aggregate scale-only curves can hide regime-specific bottlenecks. It proves that the proposed metrics can separate representation error from contact failure, latency miss, safety violation, morphology loss, and scale saturation. It proves that policies targeting physical bottlenecks can outperform additional aggregate scale in this controlled setting.

The benchmark does not prove a universal robotics scaling law. It does not estimate a real exponent for any deployed robot. It does not show hardware safety. It does not claim that every contact-rich task requires the same intervention. It is a benchmark and reporting discipline that should be tested against real robot logs.

\section{Relation To Scaling Laws}

The paper is compatible with neural scaling laws in robotics \citep{neural_scaling_robotics}. It agrees that scale can improve policies. The difference is the unit of reporting. A global curve over heterogeneous physical regimes can be smooth for the wrong reason: averaging can hide the regimes where the bottleneck changes.

The paper is also compatible with data scaling in imitation learning \citep{data_scaling_il}. Demonstrations can improve policy quality, especially in representation-smooth settings. But imitation data is not automatically a contact sensor, latency compensator, or safety controller. The curve should therefore state which physical channels are present.

Finally, the paper is compatible with embodiment scaling \citep{embodiment_scaling_locomotion}. Embodiment diversity can improve transfer. The exception is that morphology diversity is not identical to contact diversity, latency diversity, or safety diversity. A cross-embodiment curve should be stratified by the physical regime being transferred.

\section{Practical Reporting Rule}

The practical recommendation is simple. When reporting embodied scaling, include at least four regime-conditioned slices:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item a representation-smooth slice where scale should help;
    \item a contact-rich slice where force or mode-switch information is needed;
    \item a latency or safety slice where action timing or constraints dominate;
    \item a morphology-shift slice where embodiment transfer is the main issue.
\end{enumerate}

For each slice, report both success and mechanism metrics. The benchmark uses exception severity, scale saturation, and intervention recovery, but the exact names are less important than the decomposition. A curve that only reports aggregate success is not enough for embodied systems.

\section{Limitations}

The main limitation is that the full-scale suite is deterministic and synthetic. It is large and adversarial, but it is not a hardware study. The model encodes a particular hypothesis about physical bottlenecks. A different simulator or real robot dataset could change the numbers. That is why the paper emphasizes falsification criteria and claim guardrails.

The second limitation is that the oracle is too clean. It knows the bottleneck and reaches utility 1.000. A learned system would need to infer the active regime under uncertainty, and that inference may fail. The gap between the router and oracle should be read as headroom, not as a promise.

The third limitation is that the benchmark does not include every robotics regime. It focuses on contact, latency, safety, and morphology because those are common and measurable. Other exception boundaries may involve energy, wear, human preference, long-horizon memory, calibration drift, or environment change.

\section{Conclusion}

Embodied systems need scaling laws, but they need regime-conditioned scaling laws. The final full-scale benchmark shows a positive and narrow result: scale-only policies improve but saturate, while policies that expose and route around physical bottlenecks achieve much higher utility. The best non-oracle policy is a regime-aware physical router with utility 0.888. The right lesson is not to reject scale. It is to report where scale is the mechanism and where physics has become the bottleneck.
